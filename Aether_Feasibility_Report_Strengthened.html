Project Feasibility Report: Aether

A Privacy-First Student Resilience Platform

Prepared by: Aarti Sri Ravikumar

Project type: Long-term project proposal and implementation feasibility report

Date: April 29, 2026

Purpose: To evaluate whether Aether is practical, ethical, technically achievable, financially reasonable, and appropriate to begin as a phased student technology project.

1. Executive Summary

Aether is a proposed privacy-first digital platform designed to help students manage stress, build emotional resilience, reflect on their well-being, and connect with appropriate support before everyday pressure becomes a crisis. The project addresses a real gap in student support: many students experience stress, academic pressure, social difficulty, and uncertainty, but they may not seek help because of stigma, cost, fear of judgment, or not knowing where to start.

The recommended version of Aether is a non-clinical student resilience platform, not a therapy service and not a replacement for counselors, parents, guardians, teachers, or licensed mental health professionals. Its strongest feasible path is to begin with a focused minimum viable product (MVP): private journaling, mood check-ins, resilience resources, safety-aware guidance, and a carefully bounded peer-support pathway.

Final feasibility verdict: Aether is feasible and recommended for approval if it is built in phases, keeps privacy and safety boundaries at the center, avoids medical or diagnostic claims, and begins with a controlled MVP before any broader student pilot.

The project is technically achievable using the existing Aether repository structure, which already supports a modern web application stack: Next.js, React, TypeScript, Tailwind CSS, Node.js, npm workspaces, automated testing, health checks, and deployment configuration for Vercel. The most important risks are not basic technical implementation; they are responsible scope control, privacy protection, crisis safety messaging, fairness in support recommendations, and age-appropriate use.

2. Project Description and Problem Statement

2.1 The Problem

Students often face pressure from schoolwork, grades, family expectations, friendships, identity development, social comparison, financial concerns, and uncertainty about the future. Support systems such as counseling offices, trusted adults, and school resources are important, but many students delay asking for help until stress has already grown severe.

The current support gap can be summarized in four points:

  • Access gap: Students may not know where to begin or may not have immediate access to structured help.
  • Stigma gap: Students may avoid support because they fear being judged or misunderstood.
  • Prevention gap: Many tools respond after problems escalate instead of helping students build daily habits earlier.
  • Privacy gap: Students need tools that feel safe, respectful, and transparent about what is collected and why.

2.2 Proposed Solution

Aether will provide a student-friendly web experience that encourages private reflection, simple emotional check-ins, resilience-building habits, and connection to appropriate support resources. It will use plain language, calm design, and clear guardrails so students understand what the platform can and cannot do.

The platform should be designed around one principle: support students early without pretending to be a clinical system. Aether can help students notice patterns, build healthier habits, and identify next steps, but it must never diagnose, treat, or manage emergencies.

2.3 Primary Objectives

  • Build a responsive, accessible web platform that works well on phones, tablets, laptops, and desktops.
  • Offer private journaling and mood check-ins that help students reflect without feeling judged.
  • Provide structured resilience pathways with small, practical actions students can complete weekly.
  • Include safety-aware language, crisis resource direction, and clear boundaries around non-clinical support.
  • Create a foundation for future peer-support matching while avoiding unmoderated or unsafe interactions in the MVP.
  • Protect user privacy through data minimization, transparent consent language, and careful security choices.

3. Scope of the Recommended MVP

Aether is most feasible if the first version is intentionally focused. The MVP should prove the core idea without creating unnecessary safety, moderation, or compliance risk.

Included in MVP Deferred Until Later
Public informational pages explaining Aether, privacy, safety, and resilience concepts. Clinical mental health diagnosis, therapy, treatment planning, or medical advice.
Private journaling prototype with clear user control and minimal stored data. Open-ended unmoderated peer chat or student-to-student messaging.
Mood check-ins and simple trend summaries written in non-diagnostic language. High-stakes automated decisions about student risk or school intervention.
Resilience pathway content with practical habits, reflection prompts, and support-seeking suggestions. Institution-wide deployment involving minors without school approval, consent planning, and policy review.
Safety page with crisis resources and guidance to contact trusted adults or emergency services when needed. Complex AI coaching that could be mistaken for professional counseling.

Recommended scope decision: Build the first version as a resilience and reflection platform. Treat peer support and AI-assisted personalization as future enhancements that require extra safety review.

4. Market and User Feasibility

Aether fits a real user need because students already use digital tools for planning, schoolwork, communication, journaling, and wellness. Existing wellness products often focus on meditation, therapy access, habit tracking, or general mood journaling. Aether can stand out by focusing specifically on students, early support, privacy-first design, and responsible connection to peer or adult support.

The target audience includes high school and college students who want low-pressure support, as well as teachers, mentors, guardians, or school communities interested in preventive well-being tools. The user experience must feel respectful rather than clinical, calming rather than childish, and practical rather than overwhelming.

User Group Need Aether Response
Students A private, low-stigma way to reflect and find next steps. Journaling, check-ins, resilience plans, and clear support guidance.
Parents and guardians Confidence that the tool is safe, ethical, and not replacing real care. Transparent privacy, safety boundaries, and crisis-resource language.
Teachers and mentors A constructive student support resource that encourages healthy habits. Non-clinical content, reflection prompts, and responsible escalation guidance.
School leaders A scalable preventive support model with clear implementation limits. Phased rollout, measurable outcomes, and compliance review checkpoints.

5. Technical Feasibility

5.1 Recommended Technology Stack

The project is technically feasible with the current Aether repository and a modern web application stack.

Layer Recommended Choice Reason
Frontend Next.js 14, React 18, TypeScript, Tailwind CSS Supports fast, responsive, SEO-friendly web pages with reusable components and maintainable typed code.
Backend Node.js service and future API routes Fits the existing monorepo and can support health checks, future authentication, journaling APIs, and content services.
Data Start with local/static content, then add a managed database such as Postgres when private accounts are introduced Reduces early complexity while preserving a practical path to secure persistence later.
Hosting Vercel for frontend deployment; optional managed backend/database services later Vercel is well suited for a Next.js project and supports fast global delivery with low operational burden.
Testing Jest, Testing Library, TypeScript checks, linting, build checks Provides quality gates for correctness, maintainability, and confidence before deployment.
Analytics Privacy-conscious product analytics only Measures adoption and performance without collecting unnecessary sensitive information.

5.2 Existing Technical Readiness

The current Aether codebase already supports several production-friendly practices:

  • A monorepo structure with separate frontend, backend, documentation, and shared package areas.
  • A modern Next.js frontend that can support responsive pages, metadata, sitemaps, and structured content.
  • Node.js 20.x and npm workspace scripts for consistent local and deployment workflows.
  • Automated linting, type checking, testing, and build scripts.
  • Vercel deployment configuration and health-check endpoints.
  • Documentation for peer matching service contracts and future algorithm design.

5.3 Architecture Recommendation

Aether should use a phased architecture:

  • Phase 1: Static and server-rendered public pages, resilience content, safety pages, and privacy policy content.
  • Phase 2: Private journaling and check-ins with local-only or temporary prototype storage for early testing.
  • Phase 3: Authenticated accounts, encrypted-at-rest database storage, export/delete controls, and audit-friendly privacy design.
  • Phase 4: Carefully reviewed peer-support matching with safeguards, moderation planning, and consent rules.

5.4 Technical Risks and Mitigations

Risk Impact Mitigation
Scope creep from adding clinical, AI, or social features too early High Define MVP boundaries and defer therapy-like features, real-time chat, and high-risk personalization.
Privacy mistakes with sensitive journal data High Minimize collection, provide clear consent language, limit retention, and add delete/export controls before accounts launch.
Students may interpret guidance as medical advice High Use non-clinical language, visible disclaimers, and guidance to contact trusted adults or emergency resources when needed.
Peer matching could create fairness or safety concerns High Keep peer matching out of the first MVP or limit it to opt-in, moderated, non-crisis support pathways.
Deployment or dependency configuration issues Medium Use public npm registry configuration, locked dependencies, Node 20.x, and Vercel-compatible build settings.
Performance issues on mobile devices Medium Use responsive design, lightweight pages, optimized assets, and performance checks before launch.

Aether does not need to generate immediate revenue to provide value. Its early return is educational, social, and technical:

  • Demonstrates strong full-stack engineering and responsible product thinking.
  • Creates a reusable platform that can grow into a school pilot or portfolio project.
  • Supports students with practical, preventive resilience resources.
  • Builds a foundation for future grants, partnerships, school adoption, or ethical wellness products.

7. Operational and Legal Feasibility

7.1 Operational Feasibility

Operationally, Aether is feasible if it starts as a guided self-support tool and avoids workflows that require real-time clinical response. The daily operations for an MVP are manageable: maintain content, monitor application health, review feedback, fix bugs, and improve accessibility.

The design should prioritize:

  • Mobile-first navigation because many students will access the platform from phones.
  • Plain-language content written in a calm, non-judgmental tone.
  • Fast page loads and minimal friction for check-ins or journaling.
  • Accessibility targets aligned with WCAG 2.2 AA principles.
  • Visible safety guidance and crisis-resource links.

7.2 Legal and Compliance Feasibility

Aether is legally feasible as a student project if it is positioned correctly and avoids collecting unnecessary sensitive data. However, any real deployment involving students, minors, school systems, or mental health-adjacent data requires careful review.

Area Requirement or Concern Recommended Action
Medical claims The platform must not diagnose, treat, or present itself as therapy. Use clear non-clinical disclaimers and avoid diagnostic scoring.
Student privacy Journal and mood data may be sensitive. Collect only what is necessary, explain retention, and provide delete controls.
Minors Use by minors can trigger consent and school policy requirements. Do not run a live minor-facing pilot without adult, school, and policy review.
Education records School-connected deployments may raise education privacy obligations. Keep the MVP independent of official school records unless reviewed and approved.
International users Privacy laws differ by region. Start with a limited pilot audience and expand only after policy review.

This report is not legal advice. Before any live deployment with real students, the project should be reviewed by an appropriate teacher, administrator, guardian, and legal or compliance advisor if required.

10. Must-Resolve Issues Before Launch

  • Finalize privacy language and confirm that the MVP collects only necessary information.
  • Make the non-clinical scope visible throughout the experience.
  • Add crisis-resource guidance and encourage students to contact trusted adults or emergency services when appropriate.
  • Decide whether journaling data will be local-only, stored temporarily, or stored in an authenticated database.
  • Complete responsive testing across mobile, tablet, laptop, and desktop screen sizes.
  • Run linting, type checking, automated tests, and production build before final delivery.
  • Do not pilot with minors or school-connected data until consent, supervision, and policy requirements are reviewed.

11. Conclusion and Recommendation

Aether is a strong and feasible project because it addresses a meaningful student need, can be implemented with available technology, has manageable early costs, and provides a responsible path for growth. The project becomes strongest when it is disciplined: it should start as a privacy-first resilience platform, not a clinical care product or unmoderated social network.

The recommended decision is to approve Aether for development as a phased MVP. The first version should focus on public education, journaling, check-ins, resilience pathways, safety guidance, privacy transparency, and excellent responsive design. More sensitive features such as authenticated storage, AI personalization, and peer matching should be added only after the core platform has been tested and the safety model is mature.

Recommendation: Proceed with Aether. Build the MVP now, keep the scope responsible, and use privacy, safety, accessibility, and maintainability as the project's primary quality standards.

Appendix A: Acceptance Criteria for the MVP

  • The platform clearly explains its purpose in student-friendly language.
  • The homepage, resources, privacy/safety content, and mentor/support content are responsive and accessible.
  • Journaling and check-in flows use non-diagnostic wording and do not overpromise outcomes.
  • Every sensitive workflow includes privacy expectations and user control.
  • The codebase remains modular, reusable, and easy to extend.
  • Deployment works on Vercel using public package dependencies and documented environment variables.
  • All final checks pass: lint, type checking, tests, and production build.

Appendix B: Responsible Product Positioning

Aether should describe itself as a student resilience and reflection platform. It should not use language that implies therapy, diagnosis, treatment, emergency response, or guaranteed emotional outcomes. The safest product promise is:

Aether helps students reflect, notice patterns, practice resilience habits, and find appropriate support earlier.

This positioning is credible, ethical, and achievable within the proposed technical and academic timeline.
